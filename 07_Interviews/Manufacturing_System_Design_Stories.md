# Story 1 — Designing an Industrial Measurement Information System

## The Problem

Manufacturing measurement information was generated by several independent sources.

Examples included:

* Coordinate Measuring Machines (CMM)
* FARO measurement systems
* Manual inspection records

Each source produced valuable information, but the information was fragmented.

The real problem was **not** collecting data.

The real problem was that engineers could not efficiently transform measurement information into operational knowledge.

---

## Initial Constraints

The solution had to satisfy several constraints simultaneously.

* Different inspection sources.
* Different product structures.
* Large historical datasets.
* Fast report generation.
* Future scalability.
* Minimal disruption to existing workflows.

A spreadsheet-based solution would not scale.

A simple database without domain modeling would eventually become difficult to maintain.

---

## Engineering Decision

Instead of modeling physical files, I modeled the manufacturing inspection process.

The core hierarchy became:

```text
Report

↓

Group

↓

Feature
```

This hierarchy reflects how dimensional information is organized during inspection rather than how files are stored.

That decision created several advantages.

* Clear separation of responsibilities.
* Reduced redundancy.
* Historical traceability.
* Extensibility.
* Simpler reporting logic.

---

## Why This Matters

One of the most important lessons was that manufacturing information changes continuously.

New products introduce:

* new dimensions;
* new inspection plans;
* new measurement characteristics.

A rigid database would require redesign.

The selected model allows complexity to grow without changing the underlying architecture.

---

## Scalability Considerations

The objective was never to optimize only for current production.

The architecture had to support:

* additional reports;
* additional products;
* additional measurement sources;
* future analytical applications.

The database therefore became a manufacturing information platform instead of a reporting database.

---

## Business Value

The platform delivered measurable operational improvements.

* More than 50,000 measurement records centralized.
* Approximately tenfold reduction in reporting effort.
* Improved traceability.
* Better engineering visibility.
* Foundation for future analytics.

---

## Interview Message

If asked:

> Why didn't you simply store the measurements in a spreadsheet?

Recommended answer:

> Because the real challenge was not storing measurements. The challenge was designing a manufacturing information system capable of supporting traceability, reporting and future analytical needs as product complexity increased.

# Story 2 — Separating Business Logic from Data Storage

## Problem

Industrial applications often evolve faster than their underlying datasets.

If reporting logic becomes tightly coupled to storage, every new requirement forces major redesign.

---

## Engineering Decision

Separate:

* persistence;
* processing;
* reporting;
* configuration.

Each layer has a single responsibility.

This allows business rules to evolve without redesigning the complete application.

---

## Why This Decision Matters

Manufacturing environments change frequently.

Examples include:

* new inspection plans;
* new customer requirements;
* new reporting formats.

A modular architecture minimizes maintenance effort.

---

## Interview Message

> I tried to optimize for long-term maintainability rather than for the fastest initial implementation.

---

# Story 3 — Configuration Instead of Hardcoding

## Problem

Industrial software often survives for many years.

Hardcoded values eventually become expensive to maintain.

---

## Engineering Decision

Move operational parameters into configuration files whenever practical.

Examples include:

* paths;
* reporting options;
* execution behavior;
* application settings.

---

## Benefits

Configuration becomes:

* easier to maintain;
* easier to extend;
* safer to modify.

The application becomes reusable without recompilation.

---

## Interview Message

> Configuration should represent operational variability. Business logic should represent engineering knowledge.

# Story 4 — Designing a Manufacturing Decision Support System

(Project: Real-Time Quality Prioritization System)

## Initial Observation

Manufacturing teams rarely suffer from a lack of data.

They suffer from an excess of information.

The challenge is deciding where to act first.

---

## Problem

Traditional dashboards answer:

"What happened?"

Production teams usually need another answer:

"What should I investigate first?"

---

## Engineering Decision

Instead of generating another dashboard, the application prioritizes engineering attention.

The analytical engine transforms manufacturing indicators into ranked operational priorities.

The objective is not visualization.

The objective is decision support.

---

## Why This Architecture

A visualization tool without prioritization still requires engineers to interpret every metric manually.

The prioritization engine reduces cognitive load.

It highlights the highest-impact opportunities first.

---

## Business Impact

Engineers spend less time searching.

More time solving.

---

## Interview Message

> The most valuable output of the system is not the dashboard. It is the prioritization logic that converts manufacturing information into engineering actions.

# Story 5 — Why Python?

## Decision Context

The language was never the objective.

The objective was rapid development of analytical tools.

Python provided:

* fast iteration;
* analytical ecosystem;
* maintainable code;
* excellent support for data processing.

If another language better satisfied future constraints, the architecture would remain valid.

---

## Interview Message

> The architecture is the long-term asset. The implementation language is replaceable.

# Story 6 — Why Relational Data?

## Decision

Manufacturing measurement information has explicit relationships.

Reports contain groups.

Groups contain measurable characteristics.

Relationships are stable.

Values change.

Relational modeling naturally reflects this domain.

---

## Why Not Flat Files?

Flat structures duplicate information.

Duplication increases:

* maintenance effort;
* inconsistency;
* reporting complexity.

Normalization improves long-term maintainability.
